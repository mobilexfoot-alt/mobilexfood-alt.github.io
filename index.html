Complete English Grammar Guide

All main grammar topics with clear explanations, structures, and usage

Present Simple

Explanation:
Present Simple is used to talk about things that are always true or happen regularly. We use it for daily routines, habits, repeated actions, permanent situations, facts, and general truths. This tense does not describe actions happening right now, but actions that are part of normal life. It is very common when talking about work, lifestyle, preferences, schedules, and timetables. Present Simple is also used in fixed programs such as train times, school schedules, and TV programs. We often use time expressions like always, usually, often, sometimes, every day, every week, never. Do not use Present Simple for temporary actions happening at the moment of speaking.

Affirmative: Subject + base verb (+s/es for he/she/it)
Negative: Subject + do/does not + base verb
Question: Do/Does + subject + base verb?
1. When to Use the Present Simple We use the Present Simple in the following situations: 1.1 Daily Routines and Habits Actions that happen regularly or repeatedly. I wake up at 7 a.m. She drinks coffee every morning. They play football on weekends. 1.2 General Facts and Truths Things that are always true. Water boils at 100°C. The sun rises in the east. Birds fly. 1.3 Permanent Situations Things that do not usually change. He lives in London. I work as a teacher. She speaks three languages. 1.4 Timetables and Schedules Often used for public transport, classes, or official schedules. The train leaves at 9 a.m. School starts on Monday. 2. Form of the Present Simple 2.1 Affirmative Sentences Subject + base verb I work. You like music. We live here. They study English. Third Person Singular (he / she / it) For he, she, it, add -s or -es to the verb. He works. She likes music. It rains a lot here. Add -es when the verb ends in: -ch (watch → watches) -sh (wash → washes) -s (miss → misses) -x (fix → fixes) -o (go → goes) 2.2 Negative Sentences Subject + do not / does not + base verb I do not (don’t) like coffee. We do not live here. They do not understand the lesson. Third Person Singular Use does not (doesn’t) and the base verb (no -s). He does not work on Sunday. She does not like milk. It does not rain much here. 2.3 Questions Do / Does + subject + base verb? Do you work here? Do they play football? Do we need a ticket? Third Person Singular Use does. Does he live here? Does she like coffee? Does it work? 3. Short Answers Yes, I do. / No, I don’t. Yes, he does. / No, he doesn’t. Yes, they do. / No, they don’t. 4. Common Time Expressions The Present Simple is often used with: always usually often sometimes never every day / week / month on Mondays in the morning Examples: She always wakes up early. I usually go to work by bus. They never eat fast food. 5. Summary Use the Present Simple for habits, facts, routines, and permanent situations. Add -s / -es for he, she, it in affirmative sentences. Use do / does for negatives and questions. Always use the base verb after do / does.
  • I wake up at 7 a.m. every day.
  • She works in an office.
  • They do not eat fast food.
  • Do you live near here?
  • I work every day.
  • She does not like coffee.
  • Do they live here?

Present Continuous

Explanation:
Present Continuous is used to describe actions that are happening now or around the present moment. It shows that an action is temporary and in progress. We also use this tense for actions that are happening during a short period of time, even if not exactly at this moment. Present Continuous is often used to describe changing situations and temporary trends. In addition, it can be used to talk about personal future plans that are already arranged. Common time expressions include now, at the moment, today, this week, currently. Do not use Present Continuous for permanent states or general facts.

Affirmative: Subject + am/is/are + verb-ing
Negative: Subject + am/is/are not + verb-ing
Question: Am/Is/Are + subject + verb-ing?
1. When to Use the Present Continuous 1.1 Actions Happening Now Use the Present Continuous for actions taking place at the moment of speaking. I am reading a book. She is talking on the phone. They are watching TV. 1.2 Temporary Actions or Situations Actions that are happening for a short period of time, not permanently. I am living with my cousin this month. He is working on a new project. She is studying English these days. 1.3 Actions Happening Around Now Things that are happening in the present period, but not necessarily at the exact moment. I am learning to drive. They are building a new house. 1.4 Planned Future Arrangements The Present Continuous can be used to talk about future plans that are already arranged. I am meeting my friend tomorrow. We are traveling next week. 2. Form of the Present Continuous 2.1 Affirmative Sentences Subject + am / is / are + verb + -ing I am working. You are listening. He is playing football. She is cooking dinner. We are studying English. They are waiting outside. 2.2 Negative Sentences Subject + am not / is not / are not + verb + -ing I am not working. He is not (isn’t) sleeping. They are not (aren’t) watching TV. 2.3 Questions Am / Is / Are + subject + verb + -ing? Am I speaking too fast? Is she listening to music? Are they coming with us? 3. Short Answers Yes, I am. / No, I’m not. Yes, he is. / No, he isn’t. Yes, they are. / No, they aren’t. 4. Spelling Rules for -ing Form 4.1 Most Verbs Add -ing to the base verb. work → working read → reading 4.2 Verbs Ending in -e Drop the -e and add -ing. make → making write → writing 4.3 One-Syllable Verbs Ending in Consonant-Vowel-Consonant (CVC) Double the final consonant. run → running sit → sitting 5. Common Time Expressions The Present Continuous is often used with: now right now at the moment today these days currently Examples: She is working right now. I am studying English these days. 6. Stative Verbs (Important Note) Some verbs are not usually used in the Present Continuous because they describe states, not actions. Common stative verbs include: like, love, hate know, believe, understand want, need, prefer Examples: I like coffee. (NOT: I am liking coffee.) She knows the answer. 7. Summary Use the Present Continuous for actions happening now or temporarily. Form it with am / is / are + verb + -ing. Use it for planned future arrangements. Do not usually use stative verbs in this tense.
  • I am studying English right now.
  • She is working from home this week.
  • They are not watching TV.
  • Are you listening to me?
  • I am studying English now.
  • She is working this week.
  • Are they waiting outside?

Past Simple

Explanation:
Past Simple is used to talk about actions and events that started and finished in the past. The time of the action is usually known or mentioned, such as yesterday, last week, last year, or two days ago. This tense is very common in storytelling, biographies, and personal experiences. Regular verbs form the past with -ed, while irregular verbs have special forms that must be memorized. Past Simple is used when the action is completely finished and has no direct connection to the present.

Affirmative: Subject + verb-ed / irregular verb
Negative: Subject + did not + base verb
Question: Did + subject + base verb?
1. When to Use the Past Simple 1.1 Finished Actions in the Past Use the Past Simple for actions that are completed and happened at a specific time in the past. I visited my grandparents yesterday. She finished her homework last night. They watched a movie two days ago. 1.2 A Series of Past Actions Use the Past Simple to describe actions that happened one after another in the past. He woke up, took a shower, and left the house. She entered the room and sat down. 1.3 Past Habits or States The Past Simple is used to talk about habits or states in the past that are no longer true. I played football when I was a child. She lived in Paris for five years. 2. Form of the Past Simple 2.1 Affirmative Sentences Regular Verbs Subject + verb + -ed I worked late. They cleaned the house. She studied English. Spelling rules for regular verbs: Most verbs: add -ed (work → worked) Verbs ending in -e: add -d (live → lived) Verbs ending in consonant + y: change y to i and add -ed (study → studied) Irregular Verbs Irregular verbs do not follow a fixed rule. You must memorize their past forms. go → went see → saw eat → ate buy → bought Examples: I went to school early. She saw her teacher. 2.2 Negative Sentences Subject + did not (didn’t) + base verb I did not like the movie. He didn’t understand the question. They did not finish the test. Note: The main verb is always in the base form after did not. 2.3 Questions Did + subject + base verb? Did you enjoy the trip? Did she call you? Did they arrive on time? 3. Short Answers Yes, I did. / No, I didn’t. Yes, she did. / No, she didn’t. Yes, they did. / No, they didn’t. 4. Common Time Expressions The Past Simple is often used with: yesterday last night / last week / last year ago (two days ago) in 2020 when I was a child Examples: We met last year. He moved here three months ago. 5. Verb "To Be" in the Past Simple The verb to be is irregular. I / He / She / It was You / We / They were Examples: I was tired yesterday. They were happy to see you. Negative: I was not (wasn’t) ready. We were not (weren’t) late. Questions: Was she at home? Were they at the party? 6. Summary Use the Past Simple for finished actions in the past. Use -ed for regular verbs and special forms for irregular verbs. Use did / didn’t for questions and negatives. Use was / were with the verb to be.
  • I visited London last year.
  • She bought a new phone yesterday.
  • They did not understand the lesson.
  • Did you enjoy the movie?
  • I visited Paris last year.
  • She did not call me.
  • Did you see him?

Past Continuous

Past Continuous is used to describe actions that were in progress at a specific time in the past. It often sets the background for another action that happened suddenly. This tense is useful for telling stories and explaining what was happening at a moment in the past. It is also used for two actions happening at the same time in the past.

Affirmative: Subject + was/were + verb-ing
Negative: Subject + was/were not + verb-ing
Question: Was/Were + subject + verb-ing?
1. When to Use the Past Continuous 1.1 Actions in Progress at a Specific Time in the Past Use the Past Continuous to describe what was happening at a particular moment in the past. I was studying at 8 p.m. last night. She was sleeping when the phone rang. They were watching TV yesterday evening. 1.2 An Action Interrupted by Another Action The Past Continuous is often used with the Past Simple. The long action is in the Past Continuous, and the short action is in the Past Simple. I was taking a shower when the lights went out. They were playing football when it started to rain. 1.3 Two Actions Happening at the Same Time in the Past Use the Past Continuous for two actions that were happening simultaneously. She was cooking while he was cleaning. I was reading and my brother was listening to music. 1.4 Background Description in Stories The Past Continuous is used to describe the background scene in stories. It was raining, and people were running for shelter. 2. Form of the Past Continuous 2.1 Affirmative Sentences Subject + was / were + verb + -ing I was working. You were listening. He was driving. She was studying. We were talking. They were waiting. 2.2 Negative Sentences Subject + was not / were not + verb + -ing I was not (wasn’t) sleeping. He was not working. They were not (weren’t) paying attention. 2.3 Questions Was / Were + subject + verb + -ing? Was she watching TV? Were they studying at that time? Was he driving fast? 3. Short Answers Yes, I was. / No, I wasn’t. Yes, she was. / No, she wasn’t. Yes, they were. / No, they weren’t. 4. Spelling Rules for -ing Form The spelling rules are the same as in the Present Continuous: Add -ing: work → working Drop -e: make → making Double final consonant (CVC): run → running 5. Common Time Expressions The Past Continuous is often used with: while when at that moment at 7 p.m. yesterday all evening Examples: She was studying while her friends were partying. We were driving home when the accident happened. 6. Past Continuous vs Past Simple (Quick Comparison) Past Continuous: action in progress I was reading. Past Simple: completed action I read the book yesterday. Example together: I was reading when he called me. 7. Summary Use the Past Continuous for actions in progress in the past. Form it with was / were + verb + -ing. Use it to show interrupted actions and background information. Often used together with the Past Simple
  • I was studying at 8 pm.
  • They were watching TV.

Future Simple

Future Simple is used to talk about decisions made at the moment of speaking, promises, offers, and predictions based on opinion. We use will when we decide something suddenly or when we want to express willingness. It is not usually used for plans made earlier.

Affirmative: Subject + will + base verb
Negative: Subject + will not + base verb
Question: Will + subject + base verb?
1. When to Use the Future Simple 1.1 Decisions Made at the Moment of Speaking Use the Future Simple when you decide to do something at the time of speaking. I’m tired. I will go to bed now. It’s hot in here. I will open the window. 1.2 Predictions About the Future Use the Future Simple to make predictions, especially when they are based on opinions or beliefs (not evidence). I think it will rain tomorrow. She will be a great teacher. People will live longer in the future. 1.3 Promises, Offers, and Requests The Future Simple is often used to express promises, offers, or requests. I will help you with your homework. (promise) We will call you later. (promise) I will carry your bag. (offer) Will you help me, please? (request) 1.4 Threats and Warnings Stop or I will call the police. Be careful, you will hurt yourself. 2. Form of the Future Simple 2.1 Affirmative Sentences Subject + will + base verb I will work tomorrow. She will study harder. They will arrive soon. Short form: will → ’ll (I’ll, she’ll, they’ll) 2.2 Negative Sentences Subject + will not + base verb I will not (won’t) forget your birthday. He will not agree with this plan. They won’t be late. 2.3 Questions Will + subject + base verb? Will you call me later? Will she come to the party? Will they understand the lesson? 3. Short Answers Yes, I will. / No, I won’t. Yes, she will. / No, she won’t. Yes, they will. / No, they won’t. 4. Common Time Expressions The Future Simple is often used with: tomorrow next week / next month / next year soon later in the future Examples: We will meet tomorrow. She will finish the project next week. 5. Shall (Optional / Formal Use) In modern English, shall is sometimes used with I and we in questions, offers, or suggestions (mainly in British English). Shall I open the window? Shall we start the meeting? 6. Future Simple vs “Going To” (Quick Note) Will: decisions made now, promises, opinions I think it will snow tonight. Going to: plans made before now, strong evidence Look at those clouds! It’s going to snow. 7. Summary Use the Future Simple for instant decisions, predictions, promises, and offers. Form it with will + base verb. Use will not (won’t) for negatives. Use will in questions by changing word order.
  • I will help you.
  • She will not forget.

Present Perfect

Present Perfect connects the past with the present. It is used for experiences, unfinished time periods, and actions with results in the present. The exact time is not mentioned. Words like ever, never, already, just, and yet are often used.

Affirmative: Subject + have/has + past participle
Negative: Subject + have/has not + past participle
Question: Have/Has + subject + past participle?
1. When to Use the Present Perfect 1.1 Life Experiences Use the Present Perfect to talk about things you have experienced in your life. I have visited Italy. She has tried sushi. They have never seen snow. (The time is not mentioned.) 1.2 Actions That Started in the Past and Continue Now Use the Present Perfect for actions or situations that began in the past and are still true now. I have lived here for five years. She has worked at this company since 2020. We have known each other for a long time. 1.3 Recent Actions with Present Results Use the Present Perfect for actions that happened recently and have a result in the present. I have lost my keys. (I can’t find them now.) He has broken his leg. (It is still broken.) 1.4 With Unfinished Time Periods Use the Present Perfect with time periods that are not finished. today this week this month this year Examples: I have spoken to her today. We have had three meetings this week. 2. Form of the Present Perfect 2.1 Affirmative Sentences Subject + have / has + past participle I have finished my homework. You have done a great job. He has eaten lunch. She has studied a lot. They have arrived safely. 2.2 Negative Sentences Subject + have not / has not + past participle I have not (haven’t) seen him. She has not (hasn’t) called me. They haven’t completed the task. 2.3 Questions Have / Has + subject + past participle? Have you ever traveled abroad? Has she finished the report? Have they arrived yet? 3. Short Answers Yes, I have. / No, I haven’t. Yes, she has. / No, she hasn’t. Yes, they have. / No, they haven’t. 4. Past Participle Forms 4.1 Regular Verbs Regular verbs form the past participle with -ed. work → worked play → played study → studied 4.2 Irregular Verbs Irregular verbs have special past participle forms. go → gone see → seen eat → eaten take → taken 5. Common Time Expressions The Present Perfect is often used with: ever / never already yet just for / since recently / lately Examples: Have you ever been to London? She has just finished her work. I have lived here for ten years. 6. Present Perfect vs Past Simple (Quick Comparison) Present Perfect: time not stated, result matters I have lost my phone. Past Simple: time stated or known I lost my phone yesterday. 7. Summary Use the Present Perfect for experiences, ongoing situations, and recent actions with present results. Form it with have / has + past participle. Do not use it with finished time expressions (yesterday, last year). Often used with for, since, ever, never, already, and yet.
  • I have visited London.
  • She has just finished.

Past Perfect

Past Perfect is used to show that one action happened before another action in the past. It helps clarify the order of events. It is common in storytelling and explanations.

Structure: Subject + had + past participle
1. When to Use the Past Perfect 1.1 An Action Completed Before Another Past Action Use the Past Perfect to talk about an action that was finished before a second action happened. She had left when I arrived. They had finished dinner before the movie started. He had studied English before he moved abroad. 1.2 With the Past Simple The Past Perfect is often used together with the Past Simple: Past Perfect → earlier action Past Simple → later action Example: I had cleaned the house before the guests arrived. 1.3 Reported Speech Use the Past Perfect when reporting what someone said about a past action. She said that she had lost her keys. He told me he had never been there before. 1.4 Unrealized or Regretted Situations The Past Perfect can be used to talk about things that did not happen. I had hoped to see her, but she was busy. We had planned to travel, but the flight was canceled. 2. Form of the Past Perfect 2.1 Affirmative Sentences Subject + had + past participle I had finished my work. She had gone home. They had already eaten. 2.2 Negative Sentences Subject + had not + past participle I had not (hadn’t) seen the movie. She hadn’t completed the task. 2.3 Questions Had + subject + past participle? Had you met him before? Had she finished her homework? 3. Short Answers Yes, I had. / No, I hadn’t. Yes, she had. / No, she hadn’t. 4. Past Participle Forms 4.1 Regular Verbs Regular verbs form the past participle with -ed. work → worked clean → cleaned 4.2 Irregular Verbs Irregular verbs have special past participle forms. go → gone see → seen write → written take → taken 5. Common Time Expressions The Past Perfect is often used with: before after already just never by the time Examples: By the time we arrived, the show had started. She had already left before I called. 6. Past Perfect vs Past Simple (Quick Comparison) Past Perfect: earlier past action She had eaten before he arrived. Past Simple: later past action He arrived at 7 p.m. 7. Summary Use the Past Perfect to show the order of past events. Form it with had + past participle. Often used with the Past Simple. Common in storytelling and reported speech.
  • I had finished before she arrived.

Future Forms

English has several future forms. Will is used for instant decisions, going to for plans and predictions based on evidence, Present Continuous for arranged plans, and Present Simple for schedules. Choosing the correct form depends on intention and context.

Examples: will / going to / present continuous
1. Future Simple (Will) Use Decisions made at the moment of speaking Predictions based on opinions or beliefs Promises, offers, requests, warnings Form Subject + will + base verb I will call you later. She will help you. 2. Be Going To Use Plans or intentions decided before now Predictions based on present evidence Form Subject + am / is / are + going to + base verb I am going to study tonight. Look at the clouds! It is going to rain. 3. Present Continuous (Future Meaning) Use Fixed future arrangements (time and place are usually mentioned) Form Subject + am / is / are + verb + -ing I am meeting my boss tomorrow. They are flying to Paris next week. 4. Present Simple (Future Meaning) Use Timetables, schedules, and official programs Form Subject + base verb (+ -s / -es) The train leaves at 6 a.m. The class starts on Monday. 5. Future Continuous Use Actions that will be in progress at a specific time in the future Polite questions about plans Form Subject + will be + verb + -ing I will be working at 8 p.m. Will you be using the car tomorrow? 6. Future Perfect Use Actions that will be completed before a specific time in the future Form Subject + will have + past participle She will have finished the report by Friday. They will have arrived by noon. 7. Future Perfect Continuous Use To emphasize the duration of an action up to a point in the future Form Subject + will have been + verb + -ing By next year, I will have been working here for ten years. 8. Common Time Expressions for the Future tomorrow next week / month / year soon in the future by tomorrow / by next year at this time tomorrow 9. Quick Comparison Table (Summary) Will → instant decisions, promises, opinions Going to → prior plans, strong evidence Present Continuous → fixed arrangements Present Simple → timetables Future Continuous → action in progress Future Perfect → completed before a future time Future Perfect Continuous → duration until a future time 10. Summary English uses multiple future forms, not just one tense. Each form has a specific meaning and purpose. Choosing the correct future form depends on intention, planning, and context.
  • I am meeting him tomorrow.

Passive Voice

Passive Voice is used when the action is more important than who does it. It is common in formal writing, news, and instructions. The focus is on the result or process.

Structure: Subject + be + past participle
1. Active Voice vs Passive Voice Active Voice The subject performs the action. The teacher explains the lesson. They built the bridge in 2010. Passive Voice The subject receives the action. The lesson is explained by the teacher. The bridge was built in 2010. 2. When to Use the Passive Voice 2.1 When the Doer Is Unknown My phone was stolen. The window was broken. 2.2 When the Doer Is Not Important The room is cleaned every day. English is spoken in many countries. 2.3 When the Focus Is on the Action or Result The documents have been signed. The work will be finished soon. 2.4 In Formal, Scientific, or Academic Writing The experiment was conducted in a laboratory. The data was analyzed carefully. 3. Form of the Passive Voice Subject + appropriate form of be + past participle The cake is made fresh every day. The letters were sent yesterday. The report has been completed. 4. Passive Voice in Different Tenses Present Simple Passive am / is / are + past participle The office is cleaned every morning. The rules are explained clearly. Past Simple Passive was / were + past participle The meeting was canceled. The books were delivered yesterday. Present Continuous Passive am / is / are + being + past participle The house is being painted. The problem is being discussed. Past Continuous Passive was / were + being + past participle The road was being repaired. Present Perfect Passive has / have + been + past participle The work has been finished. The tickets have been sold. Future Simple Passive will be + past participle The project will be completed soon. You will be informed later. 5. Using "By" in Passive Sentences Use by when it is important to say who performed the action. The book was written by a famous author. The song was sung by her. (Often, the “by-phrase” is omitted if it is not important.) 6. Changing Active to Passive (Steps) Move the object of the active sentence to the subject position. Use the correct form of be. Use the past participle of the main verb. Add by + agent if necessary. Example: Active: She cleans the room. Passive: The room is cleaned (by her). 7. Summary The Passive Voice focuses on the action, not the doer. Form: be + past participle. Used when the doer is unknown, unimportant, or obvious. Common in formal and academic writing.
  • The house was built in 1990.

Conditionals

Conditionals describe possible, real, or imaginary situations and their results. English has zero, first, second, third, and mixed conditionals. They express cause and effect. 0️⃣ Zero Conditional Form: If / When + Present Simple, Present Simple Use: To talk about general truths, facts, and things that always happen. Examples: If you heat ice, it melts. When I eat fast food, I feel sick. If you don’t study, you fail. Result is always true. 1️⃣ First Conditional Form: If + Present Simple, will + base verb Use: For real and possible situations in the future. Examples: If I study, I will pass the exam. If it rains, we will stay at home. If you train hard, you will improve. Future is likely. 2️⃣ Second Conditional Form: If + Past Simple, would + base verb Use: For unreal, imaginary, or unlikely situations (now or future). Examples: If I had more time, I would learn programming. If I were rich, I would travel the world. If I played in LA Galaxy, I would train every day. Not real right now. Important: Use were for all subjects: If I were you… 3️⃣ Third Conditional Form: If + Past Perfect, would have + V3 Use: To talk about past situations that did NOT happen and their imagined results. Examples: If I had studied, I would have passed the exam. If he had trained, he would have won the fight. If we had left earlier, we would have arrived on time. Past is impossible to change. 4️⃣ Mixed Conditional Form (common type): If + Past Perfect, would + base verb Use: When past actions affect the present. Examples: If I had studied computer science earlier, I would be better now. If I had trained more, I would be stronger today. Past → Present result.

If + condition, result
1. Zero Conditional Use Facts, general truths, scientific facts. Form If + present simple, present simple Examples: If you heat water to 100°C, it boils. If it rains, the grass gets wet. 2. First Conditional Use Real or possible future situations. Form If + present simple, will + base verb Examples: If it rains tomorrow, we will stay at home. If she studies hard, she will pass the exam. 3. Second Conditional Use Unreal or hypothetical situations in the present or future. Form If + past simple, would + base verb Examples: If I were rich, I would travel the world. If he studied more, he would get better grades. Note: Use were for all subjects in formal English (If I were, If he were, etc.). 4. Third Conditional Use Unreal or hypothetical situations in the past. Express regret or imagine different past outcomes. Form If + past perfect, would have + past participle Examples: If I had studied harder, I would have passed the exam. If they had left earlier, they would have caught the train. 5. Mixed Conditionals Combine two time references in condition and result. Example: Past condition + Present result If I had studied medicine, I would be a doctor now. Example: Present condition + Past result If I were taller, I would have reached the shelf yesterday. 6. Key Points Zero Conditional: general truths, facts First Conditional: real future possibility Second Conditional: unreal or hypothetical present/future Third Conditional: unreal past, regret Mixed Conditionals: combine past/present/future for nuanced meaning Conditional sentences often begin with if, but the order can be reversed (result + if condition). Example: We will stay at home if it rains tomorrow.
  • If it rains, I stay home.

Modal Verbs

Modal verbs express ability, obligation, advice, permission, and possibility. They do not change form and are followed by the base verb.

Structure: Subject + modal + base verb
1. Common Modal Verbs The most common modal verbs in English are: can / could may / might must have to shall / should will / would ought to 2. General Rules for Modal Verbs Modal verbs are followed by the base form of the verb. She can swim. You should study more. Modal verbs do not add -s in the third person. He can drive. (NOT: cans) Modal verbs form questions and negatives without do/does. Can you help me? She must not be late. 3. Modal Verbs and Their Uses 3.1 Can / Could Ability I can speak English. She could read when she was five. Permission Can I use your phone? Could I leave early today? Possibility It can be dangerous. He could be at home. 3.2 May / Might Permission (Formal) May I come in? Possibility It may rain later. She might be late. (Might usually shows less certainty than may.) 3.3 Must Obligation / Strong Necessity You must wear a seatbelt. Students must follow the rules. Logical Conclusion She must be tired after the trip. 3.4 Have To External Obligation I have to work tomorrow. He has to wear a uniform. (Have to is not a true modal, but it is used like one.) 3.5 Should / Ought To Advice or Recommendation You should see a doctor. We ought to help others. Expectation The train should arrive soon. 3.6 Will / Would Will Future intentions and promises I will help you. Would Polite requests Would you open the window? Imaginary situations I would travel more if I had time. 3.7 Shall Used mainly in questions with I and we (formal or British English). Shall I start the meeting? Shall we go now? 4. Negative Forms of Modal Verbs Add not after the modal verb: cannot / can’t must not / mustn’t should not / shouldn’t may not Examples: You mustn’t smoke here. She can’t come today. 5. Modal Verbs in the Past Some modal meanings in the past use special forms: could (past ability) I could swim when I was young. had to (past obligation) We had to leave early. should have / could have + past participle You should have called me. 6. Summary Modal verbs express ability, permission, obligation, advice, and possibility. They are followed by the base verb. They do not change form. They are essential for polite and natural English communication.
  • You should study.

Reported Speech

Reported Speech is used to tell someone what another person said without using their exact words. Tenses often change.

Example: He said that he was tired.
1. Direct Speech vs Reported Speech Direct Speech She said, "I am tired." He asked, "Where are you going?" Reported Speech She said that she was tired. He asked where I was going. 2. Rules for Changing from Direct to Reported Speech 2.1 Change of Pronouns Direct: "I am happy." → Reported: He said he was happy. Direct: "You are late." → Reported: She said I was late. 2.2 Change of Verb Tense When reporting past speech, shift the tense back if the reporting verb is in the past. Direct Speech Reported Speech Present Simple Past Simple Present Continuous Past Continuous Present Perfect Past Perfect Past Simple Past Perfect Past Continuous Past Perfect Continuous Examples: "I live in London." → He said he lived in London. "She is studying." → They said she was studying. (If the reporting verb is in the present, tenses usually do not change.) 2.3 Time Expressions Time expressions often change in reported speech: Direct Speech Reported Speech today that day yesterday the day before / the previous day tomorrow the next day / the following day now then ago before last week the week before Examples: "I met him yesterday." → She said she had met him the day before. "I will call you tomorrow." → He said he would call me the next day. 2.4 Questions Yes/No Questions: use if / whether Direct: "Are you coming?" → Reported: She asked if I was coming. Wh- Questions: keep the question word Direct: "Where do you live?" → Reported: He asked where I lived. 2.5 Imperatives / Commands / Requests Use to + base verb or should + base verb Direct: "Close the door!" → He told me to close the door. Direct: "Please help me." → She asked me to help her. 2.6 Modals in Reported Speech Some modal verbs change in reported speech: Direct Reported can could may might must had to will would shall should Examples: "I can swim." → He said he could swim. "You must study." → She said I had to study. 3. Summary Reported Speech conveys someone else’s words indirectly. Change pronouns, verb tenses, and time expressions as needed. Yes/No questions use if / whether. Imperatives use to + verb. Modal verbs may change form in reported speech.

Articles

Articles define nouns. A and an are used for general meaning, the for specific meaning, and no article for general plural ideas.

a/an + singular | the + specific noun
1. Types of Articles 1.1 Definite Article: "the" Used to refer to a specific noun that is known to the speaker and listener. Used with singular and plural nouns. Examples: The book on the table is mine. The students in my class are smart. I saw the movie you recommended. 1.2 Indefinite Articles: "a" / "an" Used to refer to a non-specific noun or when mentioning something for the first time. "a" is used before words that begin with a consonant sound. "an" is used before words that begin with a vowel sound (a, e, i, o, u). Examples: I saw a cat in the garden. She is an engineer. Can I have a pen? 1.3 Zero Article Sometimes, no article is used with plural and uncountable nouns when talking generally. Examples: Dogs are loyal animals. (general statement) Water is essential for life. I like music. 2. Rules and Uses 2.1 When to Use "the" Unique objects: the sun, the moon, the Earth Specific things already mentioned: I bought a book. The book is interesting. Superlatives: the best, the tallest Ordinals: the first, the second Certain geographical names: the Pacific Ocean, the Alps 2.2 When to Use "a" / "an" One or any member of a group: a car, an apple Professions: She is a doctor. With singular, countable nouns mentioned for the first time. Expressions of quantity: a lot of, a few, a couple of 2.3 When No Article Is Needed Plural nouns when speaking generally: Cars are expensive. Uncountable nouns when general: Milk is healthy. Names of countries (except those with plural forms or words like "republic" or "kingdom"): France, Japan 3. Special Cases Some countries, rivers, seas, deserts, mountain ranges, and organizations use the: the Netherlands, the Nile, the Sahara, the United Nations. Before adjectives to refer to a whole class: the rich, the poor, the young. With certain institutions or places when talking about their purpose: go to school, be in prison, go to the hospital (British English rules may vary). 4. Summary Articles define nouns as specific (the) or general (a / an). Use the for unique, known, or previously mentioned nouns. Use a / an for one, any, or when introducing a noun. Sometimes, no article is needed with general plural or uncountable nouns.

Prepositions

Prepositions show relationships of time, place, and movement. Common ones include in, on, at, to, and from.

Examples: in time, on Monday, at home
1. Common Types of Prepositions 1.1 Prepositions of Place / Location at – specific point I am at the bus stop. in – enclosed space or area She is in the kitchen. on – surface The book is on the table. under / below / beneath – lower position The cat is under the chair. above / over – higher position The lamp hangs above the table. 1.2 Prepositions of Time at – specific time The train leaves at 6 p.m. on – days and dates My birthday is on Monday. Christmas is on December 25th. in – months, years, centuries, long periods I was born in 1990. She will graduate in June. since – starting point I have lived here since 2010. for – duration We have waited for two hours. by – deadline The report must be finished by Friday. 1.3 Prepositions of Direction / Movement to – destination We are going to the park. into – movement inside She walked into the room. onto – movement onto a surface The cat jumped onto the table. from – starting point I came from London. out of – movement from inside He ran out of the house. 1.4 Prepositions of Cause, Reason, or Purpose because of – reason The match was canceled because of rain. due to – formal reason The delay was due to traffic. for – purpose This gift is for you. thanks to – positive reason Thanks to her help, we finished early. 1.5 Prepositions of Manner, Instrument, or Agent by – agent / doer The book was written by a famous author. with – instrument / tool He cut the paper with scissors. like – manner / similarity She dances like a professional. 1.6 Prepositions of Comparison and Concession as – in the role of She works as a teacher. than – comparison He is taller than his brother. despite / in spite of – concession Despite the rain, we went outside. In spite of his illness, he attended the meeting. 2. Common Collocations with Prepositions interested in, good at, afraid of, famous for depend on, belong to, agree with, wait for keen on, similar to, proud of Examples: She is interested in music. They depend on their parents. He is proud of his achievements. 3. Summary Prepositions show relationships between words, indicating time, place, direction, cause, manner, or instrument. Prepositions often combine with verbs, nouns, or adjectives to form collocations. Using the correct preposition is essential for natural English.

Comparatives

Comparatives compare two things. Short adjectives add -er, long adjectives use more.

tall → taller | interesting → more interesting
1. Forming Comparatives 1.1 One-Syllable Adjectives Add -er to the adjective. Add more only in special cases for emphasis or clarity. Examples: tall → taller John is taller than Mike. small → smaller This bag is smaller than that one. fast → faster She runs faster than her brother. 1.2 Adjectives Ending in -e Add -r large → larger nice → nicer 1.3 One-Syllable Adjectives Ending in Consonant + Vowel + Consonant (CVC) Double the final consonant and add -er big → bigger hot → hotter thin → thinner 1.4 Two-Syllable Adjectives Usually use more + adjective careful → more careful polite → more polite 1.5 Adjectives with Three or More Syllables Use more + adjective beautiful → more beautiful interesting → more interesting 2. Comparing with "Than" Comparatives are usually followed by than to show the second item in the comparison. Examples: She is taller than her sister. This book is more interesting than that one. My car is faster than yours. 3. Irregular Comparatives Some adjectives have irregular comparative forms: Adjective Comparative good better bad worse far farther / further little less many / much more Examples: This movie is better than the last one. His situation is worse than I thought. 4. Using Comparatives in Sentences Use comparatives to show difference between two things. Optional words: much, a lot, slightly, far This exam is much easier than the previous one. He is slightly taller than his brother. Comparatives can also be used with verbs to compare actions: She works harder than I do. He runs faster than his friends. 5. Summary Comparatives are used to compare two things. One-syllable adjectives: add -er. Two or more syllables: use more + adjective. Irregular adjectives have special forms. Comparatives are usually followed by than.
